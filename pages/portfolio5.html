Different Types Of Motherboards

Introduction


A motherboard is one of the most complex components of a computer. When building a system, the choice of processor and motherboard is crucial, as these decisions dictate the compatibility of all other parts. In this comparative study, we will examine various types of motherboards, analyzing their features, differences, and suitability for different computing needs. This exploration will help to understand the unique capabilities and applications of each type, guiding informed decisions for specific use cases.



Discussion


  • Full-size AT motherboard
    • Introduced with IBM's Personal Computer AT in August 1984
    • Measures 12 inches wide by 13.8 inches deep
    • Accommodates the 16-bit 286 processor and its components
    • Enlarged PC/XT motherboard with the same screw holes and connector positions
    • Extended the board in two directions
  • ATX motherboard
    • Introduced by Intel in the mid-1990s, enhancing the AT-style architecture
    • Processor and memory slots positioned perpendicular to the expansion cards
    • Aligned with the power supply fan for better cooling
    • Accommodates full-length expansion cards
    • Standard ATX boards measure 12 x 9.6 inches (305 x 244 mm)
    • Remains the dominant motherboard type today
  • BTX motherboard
    • Introduced in September 2003 by Intel for improved cooling and circuit routing
    • Targeted at better chassis flexibility
    • Updates in 2004 and 2005, but discontinued in 2006 due to energy-efficient dual-core processors
    • Offered better airflow, support for heavy heatsinks, and scalable low-profile options
    • Shared the same power supply connectors as ATX12V
  • Extended ATX (EATX) motherboard
    • Largest ATX motherboard, designed for high-performance PCs
    • Fits full tower cases for ample space and better cooling
    • Enhances overclocking and system performance
    • Ideal for gamers and professional creators
    • Benefits include extensive memory capacity and multiple GPU support
    • May be excessive for hobbyists or users with simpler needs
  • LPX and mini-LPX form factors
    • Designed by Western Digital in 1987 for low-profile motherboards
    • Allow expansion cards to install sideways in smaller, slimmer cases
    • Boards are semi-proprietary and incompatible between manufacturers
    • Popular in budget PCs from the late 1980s to the late 1990s
    • Now obsolete due to lack of standardized specifications
  • Micro ATX motherboard
    • Designed for both standard ATX cases and compact cases
    • Retains ATX layout for better cooling, but sacrifices expansion options
    • Uses lower-wattage power supplies to minimize power and heat
    • Shares width and mounting pattern with ATX, but smaller (9.6 x 9.6 inches or 244 x 244 mm)
  • Mini-ITX motherboard
    • Shares four mounting holes and rear interface placement with ATX and micro ATX boards
    • Compatible with ATX cases, but other ITX form factors are not standardized for PC cases
  • Mini-ATX motherboard
    • Not an official standard, refers to a smaller version of ATX
    • Removed from the ATX 2.1 and later specifications
    • Generally incompatible with ATX cases despite similar hole layouts
  • Pico BTX motherboard
    • Smaller version of BTX, compact enough for micro motherboards
    • Includes an extra expansion slot for riser cards and half-height configurations
    • Initially allowed easy conversion between BTX and ATX, but now oriented differently
