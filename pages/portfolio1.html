Computer Science as a Discipline

Introduction


  • Definition and Purpose of Computer Science
    • Academic discipline focused on the systematic study of automated computation.
    • Combines theory, abstraction, and design to solve problems using fundamental conceptual principles (St. Olaf College, n.d.).
  • Historical Origins
    • Originated in the early 1940s from algorithm theory, mathematical logic, and the invention of the stored-program electronic computer (Denning, 1999).
    • Emerged as a distinct field by the late 1950s and early 1960s, driven by electrical engineers and mathematicians (Kaiser, n.d.).
  • Scope of Computer Science
    • Encompasses both theoretical and practical domains:
      • Theoretical: Algorithms, data structures, artificial intelligence, theoretical foundations.
      • Practical: Hardware design, system architecture, operating system development, database structuring (Tucker and Belford, 2024).
  • Interdisciplinary Nature
    • Incorporates principles from mathematics, engineering, and sciences.
    • Techniques used include queueing theory, probability, and hypothesis testing for algorithm and system design.
  • Programming and Beyond
    • Programming is a key component but only one part of the broader field.
    • Includes areas such as hardware design and model validation.
    • Focus on programming languages often overshadows the diverse aspects of the discipline (Denning et al., 1989).


5 Computing Disciplines and Majors


  • Computer Engineering
    • Focus on integrating hardware and software, with emphasis on embedded systems (devices with integrated software and hardware) (Conolly et al., 2017).
    • Applies electrical engineering, mathematics, and engineering principles to design computers and computer-based devices (Lunt & Ekstrom, 2008).
    • Covers topics like circuits, digital signal processing, hardware testing, fault tolerance, and mathematical foundations (Ramos-Torres, 2018).
  • Computer Science
    • Strong foundation in mathematics and algorithms, focused on finding optimal solutions and developing new technologies (Conolly et al., 2017).
    • Includes a broad range of computing topics, emphasizing theoretical aspects, while applied topics are covered by other disciplines (Ramos-Torres, 2018).
    • Areas of innovation include robotics, computer vision, intelligent systems, and bioinformatics.
  • Information Systems
    • Blends information technology with business processes, focusing on information processing and organizational functions (Conolly et al., 2017).
    • Emphasizes organizational theory, business processes, industry regulations, and application technologies (Ramos-Torres, 2018).
  • Information Technology
    • Focuses on delivering technology services to various sectors (business, government, healthcare, education) (Conolly et al., 2017).
    • Covers the selection, application, integration, and management of computing technologies (Lunt & Ekstrom, 2008).
    • Programming is included to support application development and user needs in organizational and societal contexts (Ramos-Torres, 2018).
  • Software Engineering
    • Design and maintenance of large software systems using math, computer science principles, and engineering practices (Conolly et al., 2017).
    • Focuses on applying existing knowledge to develop reliable software systems and managing complex projects (Ramos-Torres, 2018).


Analysis / Reaction


As a child, I was always curious about how computers work, which is one reason I chose computer science as my course. I want to learn more about computers, and I have no regrets about choosing this path, as I believe technology is our future. With technology evolving every day, having little to no knowledge about it makes you feel irrelevant. While researching online, I gained a deeper understanding of the vastness of the computing world. Initially, I had a basic idea of how computer science differed from computer engineering, but I didn't fully grasp their distinct focuses and roles. Additionally, though I was somewhat familiar with other fields and disciplines of computing, I now have a clearer understanding of their differences.



People often say that computer science is just about programming, and I used to think the same. However, as Peter Denning has noted, computer science encompasses far more than programming, such as designing operating system layers, structuring databases, and validating models. While programming was one of the reasons I chose this course, I've realized that there is much more to learn beyond it. Computer science is part of a larger picture. This broader perspective has not only deepened my understanding but also enlightened me that computer science is more than just programming.



Delving deeper into computer science made me realize there's much more to learn beyond programming, and it's a step-by-step process. As technology evolves daily, I understand the importance of continuous learning to stay relevant. Furthermore, learning more about computer science has increased my interest in the field. As for now, I have not decided yet on what to specialize in but exploring topics like data science, artificial intelligence, and cybersecurity has opened my mind for growth and discovery. Each new concept and technology fuels my curiosity and drives me to further discover and contribute to the ever-expanding field of computer science.
