Data, Information, and Information Systems

Introduction


Data

  • Definition: Raw, unprocessed facts or figures about people, locations, events, or objects.
  • Characteristics:
    • Lacks inherent meaning without context or processing.
    • Exists in unprocessed forms such as text, numbers, or symbols.
    • Represents coded invariances in computational systems (Zins, 2007).
    • In empirical studies, reflects statements or observations from informants.
    • Computerized representations of attributes of real or simulated entities (Chen et al., 2009).
  • Types:
    • Numeric, float, text, graphics, and sound.
    • Stored as a series of numbers for input and processing in computing systems (Das, n.d.).
  • Storage and Use:
    • Frequently stored in spreadsheets or databases (Bellinger et al., 2003).
    • Remains meaningless until processed.

Information

  • Definition: Processed and organized data, contextualized to convey meaning.
  • Characteristics:
    • Easier to interpret and understand compared to raw data.
    • May take forms such as graphs, tables, videos, or other formats (Ibrahim, 2023; Das, n.d.).
    • Data is given relational connections, which can make it meaningful (Bellinger et al., 2003).
    • Reflects the intended meaning of statements in human discourse (Zins, 2007).
  • Generation:
    • Results from computational processes such as statistical analysis.
    • Meanings are assigned to data through human or automated interpretation (Chen et al., 2009).
  • Applications:
    • Used to generate insights and inform decisions.
    • Organized in relational databases for effective retrieval and interpretation (Bellinger et al., 2003).

Information Systems


  • Definition:
    • An information system (IS) is a network of components that gather, process, store, and distribute data to aid decision-making and coordination within organizations.
    • IS integrates hardware, software, and telecommunications, particularly in business settings (Bourgeois et al., 2019).
  • Structure:
    • IS are socio-technical systems combining:
      • A technical sub-system (technology and processes).
      • A social sub-system (people and structures).
    • These components collaborate to meet objectives (Watson, 2007).
  • Functions:
    • Collecting and distributing information.
    • Providing feedback mechanisms to help businesses achieve goals like:
      • Increasing profits.
      • Improving customer service.
  • Examples of daily interactions:
    • Using ATMs.
    • Accessing information online.
    • Scanning barcodes at self-checkouts (Stair & Reynolds, 1994).
  • Applications in business:
    • Solving problems.
    • Making decisions.
    • Supporting operations, such as:
      • E-commerce.
      • Airline reservations (Oz, 2008).
  • Purpose:
    • Designed to fulfill specific objectives, such as:
      • Meeting the information needs of certain users.
      • Supporting an organization's strategic goals (Cornford & Shaikh, 2019).


Different Types of Support Systems in Information Systems


  • Transaction Processing Systems (TPS)
    • Operational-level systems situated at the base of the information system hierarchy.
    • Operated by front-line workers to collect, store, modify, and retrieve transactional data.
    • Essential for daily business operations like deposits, withdrawals, shipping, billing, and order processing (Simplilearn, 2024).
    • Automates repetitive transactions for tasks such as payroll, billing, and inventory management (Al-Tuhaifi, 2024).
    • Enhances performance, reliability, and consistency in daily operations (Mukherjee, 2024).
    • Generates reports to assist operational managers in decision-making (Florida Tech, 2024).

  • Management Information Systems (MIS)
    • Designed for middle managers to support day-to-day decision-making and performance monitoring.
    • Generates scheduled reports using data from Transaction Processing Systems (TPS) (Ali & Ali, 2018).
    • Aids in decision-making, planning, and workflow control through structured information (Simplilearn, 2024).
    • Increases productivity, efficiency, and effectiveness, saving resources like labor, money, and time (Al-Tuhaifi, 2024).
    • Facilitates performance tracking, workflow management, and managerial feedback (Mukherjee, 2024).
    • Generates sales, inventory, and financial reports from departmental data (Florida Tech, 2024).

  • Decision Support Systems (DSS)
    • Knowledge-based systems used by senior managers for decision-making and solving ill-structured problems (Sharma, n.d.).
    • Analyzes structured information to predict potential decision outcomes (Ali & Ali, 2018).
    • Enhances decision quality through predictive insights without replacing managerial judgment (Simplilearn, 2024).
    • presents data in interpretable formats like charts and tables for easy analysis (Al-Tuhaifi, 2024).
    • Integrates diverse inputs such as documents, raw data, and business models to solve complex problems (Mukherjee, 2024).
    • Commonly used for operational challenges, like projecting outcomes for new business ventures (Florida Tech, 2024).
    • Classified as knowledge management systems due to their role in facilitating information exchange (Florida Tech, 2024).

  • Executive Support Systems (ESS)
    • Strategic tools for top executives to analyze environments, identify trends, and plan long-term actions (Sharma, n.d.).
    • Directly used by executives with tailored reporting capabilities (Ali & Ali, 2018).
    • Combines features of MIS and DSS for high-level decision-making (Simplilearn, 2024).
    • Includes advanced telecommunications, computing, and display features (Al-Tuhaifi, 2024).
    • Monitors performance, tracks competitors, and forecasts trends in real-time (Mukherjee, 2024).
    • Offers customizable digital dashboards and solutions for executive needs (Florida Tech, 2024).


Analysis / Reaction


First of all, I had a hard time searching for multiple sources of information on the assigned topics. However, I was glad that most of the sources I gathered helped deepen my understanding of Information Systems. Many people, especially those unfamiliar with the difference between data and information, may think both terms refer to the same thing. It is essential to learn the distinction between the two as we encounter them in our daily lives. For example, when we use transportation apps such as Angkas, upon installing the app, we are most likely asked for our name, address, contact number, etc., which are then transformed into information. This information helps the drivers reach us quickly. This is why knowing how to distinguish between data and information makes these processes easier.



Information Systems is not entirely new to me, as I read about it once when I was preparing to compete in a quiz bowl. However, my knowledge of Information Systems was neither deep nor shallow at the time. I had some knowledge about the different types of support systems within Information Systems, and creating this portfolio helped me understand the roles, similarities, and differences between them. One key system I encountered is the Transaction Processing System (TPS). This is fundamental to any business because it handles routine transactions like billing, payroll, and order processing. From my experience, TPS has allowed for the automation of repetitive tasks, freeing up human resources for more complex activities.



In conclusion, despite the initial challenge of finding multiple sources on the assigned topics, the research process significantly deepened my understanding of Information Systems. Learning to differentiate between data and information proved essential, as it enhances the efficiency of everyday applications such as transportation apps. My prior knowledge of Information Systems was expanded further through this portfolio, particularly in understanding the various support systems. Overall, I have broadened my comprehension and appreciation of Information Systems and their impact on both businesses and daily life.
